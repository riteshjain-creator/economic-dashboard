# Indian Economic Analysis: Credible Sources & Analysts

**Last Updated:** March 28, 2026  
**Purpose:** Curated list of first-principles thinkers and credible sources for Indian macroeconomic analysis

---

## 🎯 Selection Criteria

1. **First-principles thinking** – Deep analysis from fundamentals, not surface-level trend following
2. **Track record** – Documented accuracy on major economic calls
3. **Institutional credibility** – Academic, regulatory, or research institution backing
4. **Substance over popularity** – Quality analysis over social media engagement metrics

## 🏛️ Institutional & Official Sources

### **Reserve Bank of India (RBI)** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **What to Follow:**
  - **Monetary Policy Reports (MPR):** Bi-annual, deep dive into inflation, growth outlook
  - **Bulletin Articles:** Monthly, staff research on various topics
  - **Speeches:** Governor, Deputy Governors (especially Michael Patra on inflation)
  - **RBI Database (DBIE):** Primary data source
  - **Financial Stability Report (FSR):** Twice-yearly, banking system health
- **Link:** https://www.rbi.org.in
- **Why First-Principles:** Official data, research by PhD economists, no commercial bias
- **How to Use:** Read MPR when released (April, October); track key speeches

---

### **Ministry of Finance & Department of Economic Affairs**
- **What to Follow:**
  - **Economic Survey:** Annual (pre-budget), comprehensive state-of-economy analysis
  - **Monthly Economic Review:** High-level summary
- **Why Credible:** Official data, though can have government spin on interpretations
- **Caveats:** More optimistic than independent economists; use for data, cross-check analysis
- **Link:** https://dea.gov.in

---

### **NITI Aayog (National Institution for Transforming India)**
- **What to Follow:**
  - **Discussion Papers:** On structural reforms, sectors
  - **State rankings:** Ease of Doing Business, SDG Index
- **Why Useful:** Government's policy thinking, long-term vision
- **Caveats:** Policy advocacy, not independent analysis
- **Link:** https://niti.gov.in

---

### **National Statistical Office (NSO) / Ministry of Statistics**
- **What to Follow:**
  - **GDP estimates:** Quarterly (advance, provisional, final)
  - **CPI/WPI data:** Monthly inflation
  - **PLFS:** Periodic Labour Force Survey (employment)
  - **Consumption surveys (HCES):** Household spending patterns
- **Link:** https://mospi.gov.in
- **Why Essential:** Primary data source for all macro analysis
- **Caveats:** Data quality debates exist (GDP back-series controversy, CPI base year discussions)

---

### **Securities and Exchange Board of India (SEBI)**
- **What to Follow:**
  - **Bulletins:** Market trends, investor behavior
  - **Board Meetings:** Policy changes
  - **Research by SEBI staff**
- **Link:** https://www.sebi.gov.in

## 🔍 How to Filter Signal from Noise: Framework

### **Red Flags (Likely Noise):**
- ❌ **No data cited** – Just opinions without backing
- ❌ **Cherry-picked timeframes** – "Best quarter since 1947!" (ignoring base effects)
- ❌ **No track record** – New voice with bold claims, no history to verify
- ❌ **Perma-bulls or perma-bears** – Always optimistic or always pessimistic regardless of data
- ❌ **Ad hominem attacks** – Focus on personalities, not arguments
- ❌ **Selling something** – Stock tips, course sales, crypto schemes disguised as analysis
- ❌ **Complexity as credibility** – Using jargon to sound smart without substance

### **Green Flags (Likely Signal):**
- ✅ **Primary sources** – Cites RBI data, NSO statistics, official reports
- ✅ **Historical context** – Compares to past cycles, understands patterns
- ✅ **Acknowledges uncertainty** – Uses confidence intervals, discusses risks
- ✅ **Evolves with evidence** – Changes views when data changes, not stuck to priors
- ✅ **Explains mechanisms** – Not just "what" but "why" and "how"
- ✅ **Intellectual honesty** – Acknowledges limitations, counterarguments
- ✅ **Cross-checks** – Multiple sources, triangulation

## 🧪 First-Principles Thinking: What It Looks Like

**NOT first-principles (trend-following/noise):**
> "GDP grew 8% last quarter, so India is booming! Buy stocks now!" 

**First-principles thinking:**
> "GDP grew 8% y-o-y, but:
> - 200 bps due to favorable base effect (last year's low)
> - Private capex remains weak (GFCF decomposition shows govt spending)
> - Rural wage growth lagging (MGNREGA data)
> - High-frequency indicators (GST collections, rail freight) showing slowdown
> → Growth is uneven; consumption story weakening; watch Q3 for sustained momentum"

**The difference:**
- **Breaks down headline number** into components
- **Checks consistency** across multiple indicators
- **Questions sustainability** vs. one-time factors
- **Looks at distributional aspects** (who's benefiting?)
- **Considers transmission mechanisms** (how does X lead to Y?)

**Final Note:** Indian economic discourse is heavily politicized. The best analysts rise above this by:
1. **Sticking to data**
2. **Being willing to criticize any government** when evidence warrants
3. **Avoiding motivated reasoning** (conclusion first, then cherry-pick data)
4. **Intellectual humility** – economics is hard; certainty is usually a red flag

Good luck building your signal-detection system. Start with Neelkanth Mishra's columns, track RBI's MPR, and calibrate from there. 🎯
